Personal essay

The meaning of my name (and how it shapes my life)

On the two words I was given and the long journey of earning them.

People name their children the way they place a bet. They find someone successful. They steal that name. They hope the luck travels too. My father was different. He named me “Đức Thái” while his own mother-in-law pushed back hard. She had known a Thai who died poor and miserable. Of course, he didn’t wish me to be unhappy or broke. He was not naming me after a man. He was naming me after an idea.

“Thai” comes from thông thái. It means knowledgeable. That word became the first weight placed on my shoulders before I could even read.

My parents explained that layer of meaning early. They encouraged me to read more books to live up to the name. And I did. My childhood was surrounded by books. In primary school, I would run from relative to relative firing trivia questions at them. Questions only I knew the answers to. Standing there in those living rooms, I felt tall. I felt powerful. Knowledge was power. I was certain of that.

But the house I grew up in was not only full of books. It was full of music too. Being a son of a musicology professor, I was forced to study piano for a few hours every day. Then music academy arrived alongside regular school. My hours were taken even before I woke up each morning. Studying in the daytime. Piano in the afternoon and evening. No television. No playing outside. No long lazy hours with friends talking about nothing.

Slowly, I lost the language of ordinary life. I would sit with people and didn’t know what to say. They talked about things I had never seen. I had no words that fit. The boy who once ran freely between rooms, full of noise and questions, went quiet. He found a corner. He opened a book no one else was reading. He listened to composers whose names no one else could spell. He became a stranger at every table.

I did not notice the transformation until it was already complete, long after I quit the music academy. Luckily, another form of art I had inherited from my late grandfather saved me. It was photography. Even though I’d taken many pictures before, I only started taking it seriously in Year 11. I started looking at the world carefully instead of reading about it. The light shining through small wildflowers on the pavement. The face of a stranger caught mid-laugh. The sky at the exact moment before dark. Through the lens I understood something I had missed for years. Life itself is a source of knowledge. Every hour lived with open eyes is a lesson.

I had fed myself from one small garden while entire forests grew unclaimed around me. By the time I finished high school, I had found a balance. Books and living. Studying and wandering. The first half of my name finally felt earned. But the second half was harder.

“Duc” comes from đức độ. It is an ancient word carrying umbrella meanings of virtue, righteousness, ethical behaviour and kind-hearted. Together, “Đức Thái” means a person who uses knowledge to do good things and help people. But how can I do that? I asked myself for years whether I was enough. Whether I knew enough. Whether any of what I had learned could truly serve someone else. I turned to research for answers. If I could verify a belief, test it, trace it back to its roots, then sharing it would not be arrogance. It would be a gift.

Then my colleagues showed me something I had not expected. They did not come to me because of my doctorate. They came because they trusted me. Because I had lived what I was talking about. Because I had tested it myself and carried the proof in my hands. The knowledge had become something real. The name was beginning to mean something in the world. I cannot yet measure the impact. I cannot see every ripple in the water. But I believe the ripples are there.

This essay does not complete the story. It does not settle the question of who I am or whether I have earned my name. It is only a record of a man figuring things out, slowly, one season at a time. I no longer chase my parents’ expectations. It is my own journey now, my own name to earn. Someday, I will sit across from a child of my own and choose their name the way my father chose mine. Not after a person but an idea. And I hope that idea will carry them forward long after I am gone, the way my name has carried me.

P/s: the second meaning of “Thái” is slice, and I have been living up to that name ever since I picked up my first kitchen knife.
